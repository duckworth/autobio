# Clean Transcript
I am backfilling the Brooklyn Music story.

My friend Brian Rath had seen a picture of Chris Donnan on the Brooklyn Music website and realized it was the same Chris who had grown up in our neighborhood and gone to school with us. We reached out to him and made a trip into Brooklyn to visit him. Chris was living there with his roommate Eric, and they had a small music studio set up in the apartment.

That visit clicked for a few reasons. Brian was deeply into music and could play keyboard, guitar, and drums. I was also into music, especially from the DJing period with my friend Gwen after I came back from Florida. We were buying vinyl, going to DJ Records on Deer Park Avenue, and getting into progressive house, techno, and rave music. So when we got to Chris's place, the conversation naturally moved from music into computers, graphic design, programming, dynamic websites, and databases.

At the time I was doing graphic design work and learning 3D modeling, Photoshop, and Illustrator. One of Duncan's friends from East Islip High School had ended up working with 3D Studio Max, so that kind of work felt current and exciting. Chris and I got into a deeper conversation about programming and web systems, and that led to more contact afterward.

I first got involved with Brooklyn Music because they were building a music player. I used 3D Studio Max and Photoshop to design a realistic web-based player that looked like a rack-mounted piece of gear, with an LCD screen, buttons, handles, screws, glowing states, and sliced-up interface parts so text and controls could be layered into it. That project pulled me into the work.

From there I started going into the Brooklyn Music studio, meeting Chris's boss and eventually Joe Natoli, one of the owners. Joe was part of the electronic duo Atomic Babies. I was still working at the boatyard and paying for Suffolk Community College, but then suddenly it became real: I was moving to Brooklyn for entry-level pay to do web design and web programming.

Chris and I went heads-down. We read constantly, learned everything we could, and built our own rack of servers. We crawled the web for electronic music websites and built a search engine called `e-Natronic`. We hosted it on our own machines and later set up equipment in a co-location facility at Globix in New York. We also built out a broader electronic music portal with CD reviews, artist bios, and related content.

That period ran roughly from 1999 into 2001. I first lived in Bensonhurst with roommates Bruce and Henry. Henry eventually moved back to Long Island, and Brian ended up taking his room. Chris rented the basement apartment of the same house, so we were all effectively living in the same place for a while.

After that first year, we moved closer to Brooklyn Heights because the Bensonhurst commute on the N train was long. Brian, Bruce, and I got a place around Henry and DeGraw. It had a huge basement where we could set up music equipment, DJ, and hang out. Chris and Shannon also moved nearby, and our friend Rod, the graphic designer, lived a block over with his girlfriend Renee. It became a tight little cluster of friends who all worked around the same creative and technical scene.

At Brooklyn Music we also built electronic music radio for Yahoo Music. We ripped the catalog Brooklyn Music had, created dynamic streaming playlists, and built out the web and backend pieces needed to make that work.

That run ended when the dot-com period collapsed. The company was running tight on money, Joe's uncle the attorney was funding much of it, and eventually everything became paycheck to paycheck. I was in Brooklyn without knowing how I was going to make next month's rent. That was the moment Chris and I looked up and realized we were actually good at this. We knew programming, databases, XML, web systems, and streaming audio well enough to get real software jobs. That became the bridge into the next phase of my career.
